What this curriculum builds on

This curriculum builds on open-source libraries, freely-licensed fonts, and Unicode emoji. No images, audio, or video are embedded from third parties. Anything that is used is listed below with the source and license.

If you redistribute or fork this curriculum, please keep this page intact and add your own attribution as appropriate.

illustrations

Illustrations

All illustration is done with Unicode emoji and CSS — no images are embedded. Recipe-card icons, ingredient symbols, decorative accents in the hero band, and Pick-tab gallery glyphs are all rendered from the standard Unicode emoji set, which means the visual appearance varies slightly depending on the operating system rendering it (Apple Color Emoji on macOS / iOS, Segoe UI Emoji on Windows, Noto Color Emoji on Android / Linux, etc.).

Unicode emoji are part of the Unicode Standard (Unicode, Inc.) and are free to use as text characters. Vendor-specific emoji rendering is the trademark and property of the respective vendor (Apple, Microsoft, Google, etc.) and is rendered by your operating system, not embedded by this curriculum.

Cooking-tradition attributions

  • The "off-and-on heat" scrambled egg method is associated with Gordon Ramsay, popularized via his TV programmes.
  • The "scrambled cream" bain-marie method is from the late Joël Robuchon, who served it with caviar in his Paris restaurants.
  • Regional dishes (Chinese tomato eggs, Japanese silken, huevos a la Mexicana, anda bhurji) are traditional cuisines of their respective cultures, not attributable to a single creator.
  • The American diner-style cooking method has no single attributable origin — it's a folk technique that emerged across mid-20th-century American short-order kitchens.

Editorial note: Where the prose uses phrases like "in my experience" or "most cooks find", that's the author's working opinion rather than a factual claim, and isn't cited. Where specific numbers, dates, names, or chemistry appear, a source is linked.
