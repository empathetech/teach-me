Carbs: bread, potatoes, hash

Learn · 5 min read

The starch is the second-most-important thing on the plate after the protein. It's also the part most cooks pick on autopilot — toast, because there's bread in the house. But the carb choice changes the whole feel of the meal.

This lesson walks through the four common options, what each one signals, and how to match them to the egg style you've picked.

The four common carbs

Most home brunches reach for one of these. Each does a different job.

🍞

Bread / toast

The default. Cheap, fast, fail-proof. A vehicle for whatever's around it.

Best with: any style. Especially diner.
Effort: 2 min
Cost: ~$0.40/p

🥔

Smashed potatoes

Boil-then-roast. Crispy outside, fluffy inside. The "wow" carb.

Best with: French soft-curd, Robuchon.
Effort: 35 min (mostly passive)
Cost: ~$0.80/p

🍳

Hash browns

Grated potato, pressed thin, fried crispy. Pure diner. Smells incredible.

Best with: American diner, Mexicana.
Effort: 15 min
Cost: ~$0.60/p

🥐

Biscuits / scones

Flaky, buttery, slightly sweet. Feels indulgent without much labor.

Best with: diner, country-style.
Effort: 25 min from scratch, or 0 if store-bought.
Cost: ~$0.70/p

The matching logic

Each carb pairs naturally with certain egg styles. The rule of thumb: match texture contrast, not texture sameness.

Soft-curd eggs are creamy and yielding. They want a contrast — something crispy and toothsome to balance them. Smashed potatoes, with their fluffy interior and crackling exterior, are perfect. Toasted sourdough is a budget version that works.

Diner-style eggs are firm and substantial. They want something that complements rather than competes. Hash browns, which share the same fried-and-firm register, are the classic American move. Biscuits work for the same reason — they're substantial without being delicate.

Chinese tomato eggs want something starchy and absorbent to soak up the sauce. Rice is traditional. A pile of buttered toast also works (cross-cultural but real).

For Robuchon's pourable egg cream, the carb is almost a serving vehicle: a slice of toasted brioche or sourdough to spoon the eggs onto. Smashed potatoes are too rustic for this style.

Bread, specifically

Of the four, bread is the lever most worth pulling. A piece of supermarket white bread costs maybe $0.15. A slice of a good country sourdough loaf costs maybe $0.90. The price gap is meaningful but small. The taste gap is enormous.

If you do one upgrade in your brunch, this is the one. Get a good loaf from a real bakery. Cut a thick slice. Toast it generously. Butter it while it's still hot. The whole meal lifts.

Toast lives in plate-immediately territory. The minute it comes out of the toaster, it's losing crunch. Time it so it lands seconds before serving. Pre-toasted-and-waiting toast is sad bread.

The "two carbs" temptation

Avoid doubling up. Toast and hash browns and biscuits is a starch-bomb plate that overwhelms the protein and leaves no room for the fresh thing. Pick one.

The exception: a tiny accompaniment that isn't really a "carb" in the meal-balance sense. A handful of crackers next to a cheese plate. A small slice of fruit bread as dessert. Those don't count.

Smashed potatoes — the technique

This is the carb I'd default to if I had 35 minutes and wanted to impress someone.

  1. Heavily salt water, bring to boil. Salt level: should taste like the ocean.
  2. Baby potatoes (about 200g per person), in. Boil until fork-tender — usually 15 minutes for small ones.
  3. Drain. Lay on a sheet pan with space between them.
  4. Press each one with the bottom of a glass until it's about ½ inch thick. Some skin will tear; that's fine — those bits will get extra crispy.
  5. Olive oil drizzle, salt, into a 425°F oven.
  6. Roast 18 minutes, flip them halfway through if you remember.
  7. Out, chopped parsley over the top, into a serving dish.

The key is the smash. A pale boiled potato has nowhere for crispy edges to form. A smashed one has a ridged surface with many small crusts. That texture is what makes the dish.

The boil-then-smash-then-roast technique is a relatively recent move — popularized in the early 2000s. The Australian food writer Jill Dupleix is widely credited with the "crash hot potatoes" version that swept food blogs. Before that, "smashed potatoes" usually meant skin-on mashed potatoes. The crispy-fried variant is younger than the iPod.

recap

What to take away

  • Four carbs to choose from: toast, smashed potatoes, hash browns, biscuits.
  • Match the carb to the egg style — contrast textures, don't duplicate them.
  • Soft-curd eggs → smashed potatoes or great toast. Diner eggs → hash or biscuits.
  • If you upgrade one thing in the brunch, make it the bread. Real bakery loaf, thick slice, generous butter.
  • One carb per meal. Don't double up.
  • Smashed potatoes are 35 min mostly passive — the cost-effective "wow" choice.
