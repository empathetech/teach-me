Salt timing & the water question

Learn · 5 min read Pairs with every egg recipe in Cook

If the first lesson was about heat, this one is about water. Eggs are mostly water — about 75% of an egg by weight. The whole game of scrambling them is about controlling how much of that water stays trapped in the curd and how much escapes onto your plate.

Salt is the variable you can dial. It has nothing to do with seasoning here — well, almost nothing. The flavor part takes a pinch. The timing part is structural.

What salt does to a raw egg

Salt is a chaotropic agent. That's a scary word for "it disrupts protein folding." When you add salt to raw beaten eggs, the salt ions weaken the bonds that hold the egg proteins in their folded shape. The proteins start to relax and unspool a little, even at room temperature.

If you cook them immediately, this doesn't matter — heat would have unfolded them anyway. But if salt sits on raw eggs for more than a few minutes, two things happen:

  • The proteins partially denature without heat. When you then cook them, they set faster and tighter.
  • The salt draws water out of the proteins through osmosis. That water has nowhere to go but the bowl, and now it's diluting the egg.

Result: eggs that weep on the plate, sit in a pool of cloudy liquid, and have a slightly tough texture.

"At the end" means within the last 10 seconds of cook time, or right when you plate. Either is fine.

So how should you actually do it?

There are three reasonable approaches. They each work, and which one fits depends on the texture you want.

1. Salt at the very end — soft-curd default

Cook the eggs in their natural protein structure. Salt the finished curd before plating. This gives the silkiest texture and the most distinct egg flavor. Best for: French soft-curd, Robuchon, Japanese silken.

2. Salt 15 minutes before — diner pre-salt

This is the surprising one. J. Kenji López-Alt's experiments show that salting the raw eggs and letting them sit a full 15 minutes before cooking gives noticeably more tender curds than salting immediately before. The salt breaks down proteins enough to form a "net" that holds moisture in as the eggs cook — so counterintuitively, the long sit produces a texture closer to soft-curd than the rushed 2-minute pre-salt. Best for: American diner-style, when you have time to plan.

3. Salt in the cook — the "as you go" method

A pinch right before you scramble. Splits the difference. Doesn't have time to draw out water meaningfully, and gives even seasoning. Best for: any egg style when you're in a hurry. The boring safe answer.

The one to avoid: Salting raw eggs 2–5 minutes before cooking. Too long to be neutral, too short to fully relax. You get all the water loss with none of the texture benefit. Either salt immediately and cook, or salt and wait the full 15.

The "should I add water or milk or cream?" question

Different liquid, similar logic. Anything you add to raw eggs is changing the water-to-protein ratio.

  • Cream (1 tsp/egg) — adds fat, which lubricates the protein mesh. Eggs set at a slightly higher temperature, giving you more margin. Silkier mouthfeel. Recommended for soft-curd styles.
  • Milk (1 tsp/egg) — adds water with a little fat. Eggs fluff up more but weep more easily if overcooked. Classic diner addition.
  • Water (1 tsp/egg) — purely lightens. Makes eggs fluffier and lower in protein concentration per bite. The chef's-trick version, but you lose some richness.
  • Crème fraîche (½ tsp/egg, off heat) — added at the end, never during cooking. Cools and enriches without diluting. Ramsay does this, Robuchon does this.
  • Nothing — the French classical answer. Just eggs, butter, salt. The eggs themselves are doing the talking.

The reason restaurant scrambled eggs taste different from yours: most kitchens add a small amount of egg yolk emulsion — extra yolks stirred into the raw egg before cooking. It boosts richness without diluting. Try cracking 6 eggs but adding 2 extra yolks next time. You'll taste it.

The order of operations, distilled

  1. Crack eggs into a cold pan with butter (or hot pan if going diner-style).
  2. Optional: add cream, milk, or nothing — your choice based on the style.
  3. Cook by the technique appropriate to the style.
  4. Off the heat before they look done.
  5. Salt now. Fold once or twice. Plate immediately.

That last step is the whole point of this lesson. Salt is the final act, not the first one.

recap

What to take away

  • Salt disrupts protein folding and pulls water out. Time it accordingly.
  • Best default: salt at the very end. Within 10 seconds of plating.
  • Alternative for soft-fluffy diner style: salt 15 minutes ahead and let it fully relax.
  • Avoid the in-between: 2–5 minute pre-salt is the worst of both worlds.
  • Cream, milk, water, crème fraîche — each changes the texture. Cream and crème fraîche are the move for richness.
