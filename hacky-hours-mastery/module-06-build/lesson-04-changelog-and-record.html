Changelog & keeping a record

When a milestone is done, Step 4 has you write down what changed and cut a tagged release. It's a small habit with a big payoff — even when you're the only person on the team. Let's see why a record matters, and how to write a clean changelog entry.

What a changelog is

A changelog is a plain file — CHANGELOG.md — that lists, in human language, what changed in each version of your app. Not the code; the story of the code, written for people. Each release gets a heading with its version number and a short list of what's new, fixed, or different.

The skill ties this to milestones: you append a milestone entry to the changelog when your backlog is empty, then tag the release. Recall a tag is a permanent label on a released version — like v0.1.0 for your MVP.

Why a record matters — even solo

It's tempting to skip this when no one else is watching. Don't. A record pays you back later:

  • You forget. In three months, "why did I change that?" has a written answer.
  • Bugs need history. When something breaks, the changelog tells you what changed and roughly when — your first clue.
  • It's a paper trail. Your commits and pull requests already record every change; the changelog turns that raw history into a readable summary anyone (including future-you) can follow.
A "paper trail" in plain terms Every commit and pull request you make is automatically dated and saved. Together they're an honest, time-stamped record of how your app came to be — proof of what changed, when, and why. The changelog is the friendly cover summary on top of that record.

Solo doesn't mean no record. Future-you is the teammate you're writing it for.

Added, Fixed, Changed

A widely-used convention (called Keep a Changelog) groups entries by type. The three you'll reach for most:

  • Added — a new feature or capability.
  • Fixed — a bug that's now repaired.
  • Changed — existing behavior that now works differently.

Each line is short, present-tense, and written for a person, not a machine — the same instinct as a good commit message.

Growing your project memory 🌱

Good next steps
  • Read Keep a Changelog — the short, plain convention behind Added / Fixed / Changed.
  • Revisit semver.org to connect each changelog version to its version number.
  • Browse a project you admire on GitHub and read its changelog top to bottom — it's the story of the whole app in a few screens.

What you just learned

  • CHANGELOG.md is the human-readable story of what changed in each version.
  • Step 4 updates it on milestone completion, then cuts a tagged release.
  • A record matters even solo: you forget, bugs need history, and commits/PRs are your paper trail.
  • Entries are grouped Added / Fixed / Changed, each a short present-tense line.
