Reading what the AI wrote

You don't have to write code to take responsibility for it. You do have to understand it well enough to say yes. Here's how a non-engineer reviews AI-written code without fear — and the one rule that keeps you safe: never merge what you don't understand.

8 min read hands-on grows: code-reading confidence
The one rule, up front Never merge a change you don't understand. The AI wrote it, but the moment you approve it, the responsibility for what it does is yours. "I didn't read it" is never a defense if your app later loses someone's data. Understanding doesn't mean reading every character — it means being able to say, in your own words, what the change does and why.

It's normal to feel out of your depth looking at code someone — or something — else wrote. The good news: you have 🖥️ Marcus Rivera (your Frontend agent) and ⚙️ Sam Park (your Backend agent) right there, and they'll explain anything you ask. Reviewing isn't about reading faster. It's about asking the right questions.

Step one: ask for plain English

Before reading a single line, ask the agent to narrate the change for you:

Then compare what it tells you to what you expected. If the explanation surprises you — it touches something you didn't ask about, or skips something you did — that gap is your signal to dig in. You're not checking the syntax; you're checking the intent.

Step two: test the user journey

This is the part you're uniquely good at. A user journey is the real sequence of steps a person takes — sign up, log in, book a walk. Open the app and actually walk through it, the way your user would. Code can look perfect and still feel broken to a real person.

You own item five Remember the pre-merge checklist: "Change has been manually tested against the relevant user journey." That's often the hardest item for the AI to truly confirm — and the easiest for you. Clicking through the real flow is a genuine engineering contribution.

Step three: watch for the security smells

From Module 4, you already know what a few risky shapes look like. You don't need to be a security expert to spot them — just to recognize them and ask:

  • A real password, key, or token typed directly into the code.
  • Input from a user being used without any check on it.
  • Error messages that dump internal detail back to the screen.

Reviewing AI code isn't about catching everything. It's about never approving something you couldn't explain to a friend.

Growing your code-reading confidence 🌱

Good next steps
  • Work through a little of MDN's Learn web development — even an hour makes the AI's explanations click faster.
  • Pick one change you merged recently and ask your team to re-explain it. Re-reading with fresh eyes is how reading gets easier.
  • Keep a running list of words you didn't know and ask the team to define each. Your vocabulary is your confidence.

What you just learned

  • Ask the agent to explain a change in plain English, then compare it to what you expected.
  • Manually test the real user journey — your strongest contribution to the review.
  • Watch for the security smells from Module 4: secrets in code, unchecked input, leaky errors.
  • Never merge what you can't explain in your own words.
  • Good questions beat expert reading — that's a skill you already have.
