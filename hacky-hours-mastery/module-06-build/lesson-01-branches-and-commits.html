Branches & commits (Step 4)

Step 4 is where your design becomes a working app. The team builds it one small, reviewable change at a time — and a few simple habits keep your work safe while you do. Let's learn the three words you'll see most: branch, commit, and merge.

8 min read hands-on grows: version-control literacy

In Step 4 — Build — the team picks one task at a time from your backlog (a to-do list of work to build), writes the code, checks it against your design, and only then calls it done. The skill describes the loop as: pick a task, make a branch, implement, verify, commit, open a pull request, then merge. Three of those words are worth learning slowly.

Branch — a safe parallel copy

A branch is a separate copy of your project where you can make changes without touching the main, working version. If the change goes wrong, your main copy is untouched — you just throw the branch away. When the change is good, you fold it back in.

Branches are named for the task. The skill suggests names like feat/user-signup (a new feature) or fix/login-error (a bug fix). The prefix tells you at a glance what kind of change it is.

The main branch stays working The idea is that your main branch always holds a version that works. All the messy, in-progress work happens on a separate branch first.

Commit — a saved checkpoint

A commit is a saved checkpoint of your work, with a short message describing what changed. Each commit is a point you can return to. Together, your commits form a clear, dated history of how the project came to be.

The message matters more than beginners expect. A good message tells future-you (and any reviewer) what changed and why, in plain present tense.

Merge — folding the work back in

To merge is to fold a finished branch back into your main copy, so the change becomes part of the real app. Before merging, the skill has you open a pull request (often shortened to PR) — a request to review and accept the change. Even working solo, a PR gives you a moment to look at everything together before it's final.

The last word: a tag. When a milestone is complete, you mark that exact version with a label like v0.1.0. A tag is a permanent bookmark on a release. We'll come back to tags and the changelog later in this module.

Why this protects you

These habits aren't bureaucracy — they're a safety net for someone who can't read every line of code at expert speed:

  • A branch means a bad change can't break your working app — you can always discard it.
  • Commits mean you can step back to any earlier checkpoint if something goes wrong.
  • A pull request gives you one clear place to review what the AI built before it becomes permanent.

You don't have to trust the AI blindly. The branch-and-PR habit gives you a spot to look before you leap.

Growing your git literacy 🌱

Good next steps
  • Read GitHub's gentle overview, About Git — it explains branches and commits without assuming you're an engineer.
  • Ask your team mid-build: "Show me what's on this branch and explain it like I'm new." It will, and you'll learn faster than from any tutorial.
  • Watch your own commit history grow on GitHub — it's the most honest record of your progress you'll ever keep.

What you just learned

  • A branch is a safe parallel copy; feat/ and fix/ name what it's for.
  • A commit is a saved checkpoint with a clear, present-tense message.
  • A merge folds a finished branch back into your working app — after a pull request review.
  • A tag permanently marks a released version.
  • These habits let you review and undo — your safety net while building.
