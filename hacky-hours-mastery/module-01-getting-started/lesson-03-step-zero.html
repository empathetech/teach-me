Step 0 — exploring safely

If you've been a little nervous to actually run anything — worried you might break something or commit to a path you can't back out of — this lesson is for you. Step 0 exists precisely so you can look around first and touch nothing.

6 min read hands-on

You've installed Hacky Hours and picked your mode. Now comes the part people quietly dread: pressing a key and seeing what happens. Good news — Hacky Hours has a setting built exactly for cautious first contact.

What Step 0 is

Most of the framework's steps eventually write files to your project — your ideation notes, your design docs, and so on. Step 0 is different: it explores without writing files. The skill's own help text describes it in five words: "Explore without writing files — safe to try anytime."

When you run it, Hacky Hours surveys your project — it reads what's there to figure out where you are — and then walks you through what it found and what you could do next. But it's in what's called dry-run mode: a mode where it shows you what it would do instead of actually doing it. Anywhere it would normally save a file, it instead displays the contents on screen with a little note like ↳ would write to <path>.

Why it's safe to try right now

Here's the reassurance, stated plainly: running Step 0 changes nothing on your computer. No files are created, edited, or deleted. Nothing is committed to git. Nothing is sent anywhere or charged to a paid account. You are simply looking around.

You can open the door, peer inside, and walk back out — and the room is exactly as you left it.

This matters because the biggest barrier for most new builders isn't capability — it's the fear of doing something irreversible. Step 0 removes that fear. You can run it as many times as you like, on any project, just to see what Hacky Hours notices.

One honest clarification Step 0 not writing files is a real, designed behavior of the framework. But "safe to explore" is not the same as "safe to ship." Once you move past Step 0 into the steps that do write files and build software, the responsibility to review what's produced — and to verify it does what you intend — is yours. Step 0 is a safe place to start, not a guarantee about everything that follows.

Now do it for real

That's the practice version. Here's your real assignment — and it's genuinely safe to do right now, in your own terminal, while this lesson is open beside you.

🛠️ Try it in your terminal
  1. Run /hacky-hours on its own and read how it greets you and describes what it found.
  2. Run /hacky-hours help and skim the full list of commands. You don't need to understand them all.
  3. Run /hacky-hours step 0 and let it survey a project (or an empty folder) without writing a thing.
Then sit with one question: did anything change on your computer? (It didn't.) Notice how that feels — that's the confidence Step 0 is meant to build.

If you ran into the first-run setup the very first time — a quick "welcome to v4" that creates a small private folder at ~/.hacky-hours/ for your settings and team — that's expected. It happens once per machine, it's local and private, and then control returns to whatever you were doing.

Going deeper 🌱

The instinct behind Step 0 — try things in a safe, reversible space before committing — is one of the most valuable habits in all of software, far beyond Hacky Hours.

A door worth walking through The biggest real-world version of "safe to explore" is version control — the ability to undo, branch, and experiment without fear. If you've never learned it, the free, beginner-friendly "Start your journey" guide from GitHub is a gentle on-ramp. Once you trust that you can always undo, your whole relationship to building changes.

What you just learned

  • Step 0 explores your project without writing files — "safe to try anytime."
  • It runs in dry-run mode: it shows what it would write instead of writing it.
  • Running it changes nothing — no files, no commits — so it's the perfect first move for a nervous beginner.
  • Your real assignment: run /hacky-hours, /hacky-hours help, and /hacky-hours step 0.
  • "Safe to explore" isn't "safe to ship" — once you move into file-writing steps, reviewing the output is on you.
