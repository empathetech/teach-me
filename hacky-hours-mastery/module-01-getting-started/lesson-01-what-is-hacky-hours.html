What Hacky Hours actually is

Before you install anything or type a single command, it's worth knowing what you're actually picking up — and, just as honestly, what it can't do for you. This is the lesson that makes everything after it make sense.

8 min read hands-on grows: building literacy

Hacky Hours is a framework — a repeatable way of working — for building real software with the help of a large language model (an "LLM," the kind of AI that powers Claude). It's a community project from Empathetech, and it lives inside Claude Code as a skill — a packaged set of instructions you call with a slash command. You'll be using version 4.0.1 throughout this course.

It exists for one reason: typing "build me an app" into an AI gets you something that looks finished but skips most of the thinking a real team would do. Hacky Hours puts that thinking back in.

The one idea everything rests on

If you remember nothing else, remember this: you drive the product; Claude helps you build it — but it needs your direction. The framework is explicit that the human is the decision-maker. As the skill's own guiding values put it, "Human is the driver. You are the implementation team. The user is the C-suite. Ask first, act second."

You bring the vision and the judgment. The framework brings the process and the hands.

That means it will ask you questions instead of guessing, and it will pause before doing anything hard to undo. It is not a button that produces a finished app while you watch.

The five steps

Hacky Hours organizes a build into five steps, in order. You move through them at your own pace, and you can revisit earlier ones whenever your idea shifts:

  1. Ideate — get the idea out of your head and into clear, written form: who it's for, what it does, and why it matters.
  2. Design — decide how the product actually works, in enough detail that someone new could understand it.
  3. Roadmap — prioritize ruthlessly, so the first version you ship is small enough to actually finish.
  4. Build — implement it in small pieces, checking each one against your design before calling it done.
  5. Iterate — gather feedback, update your plans, and queue up what's next.

These names and order come straight from the skill, which guides you "through five steps: Ideate → Design → Roadmap → Build → Iterate, so you figure out what to build before writing any code."

The team of twelve

The part that makes Hacky Hours unusual is its persistent team of twelve AI agents, each playing a real stakeholder role you'd find on a software team. Rather than one generic assistant, you get distinct voices — Product, Design, Architect, Frontend, Backend, Security, Ops, QA, Accessibility, Licensing, Data, and AI/ML — that weigh in at the right moments.

Here's the honest reframe worth holding onto: each agent is a doorway into a real discipline. When the accessibility agent flags something, that's a nudge toward a field with decades of research behind it. The agent gives you a starting point; the discipline is where the real depth lives. You'll meet all twelve and explore them yourself in the team browser.

Why a "team" and not just a smarter assistant Splitting the work into roles forces questions to the surface that a single helpful voice tends to skip — like "who can legally use this?" or "what happens to this user's data?" The roles don't replace experts; they make sure the question at least gets asked.

It picks the safe, simple path by default

Hacky Hours has a set of values baked into every recommendation it makes — you don't have to ask for them. A few of the most useful:

  • Free before paid — it suggests free and open-source tools before commercial ones, and flags costs clearly when a paid option comes up.
  • Less infrastructure before more — it prefers simple, managed solutions over things you'd have to run and maintain yourself.
  • Privacy-preserving before data-rich — it nudges you to collect the minimum data you need, and treats data collection as a deliberate decision rather than a default.

These are sensible starting points, not magic. They tilt you toward choices that are usually cheaper, simpler, and easier to keep safe.

What this lesson is not promising Hacky Hours gives you direction — good defaults, the right questions, a structured process. It does not guarantee that what you build is secure, private, legally compliant, or correct, and neither does this course. The responsibility to review, understand, and verify what you ship stays with you. The framework's job is to help you make better decisions, not to make them disappear.

It's genuinely for everyone

One of the framework's stated values is simply: "This is for everyone. From first-time builders to seasoned engineers. Never assume prior knowledge. Explain jargon the first time you use it."

Here's the clever part. The framework adapts to how much you already know: it "stands in for more of the process for someone without the background, less for someone with it." So if you're not an engineer, it does more of the heavy lifting and explains more along the way. If you are, it gets out of your way. You'll set that up in the next lesson.

Going deeper 🌱

Hacky Hours is one opinionated take on a much older idea: that deciding what to build is most of the work, and that good software comes from a process, not a burst of typing.

One door worth walking through If the idea of "figure it out before you build it" sparks something, read Joel Spolsky's classic "Painless Functional Specifications" — a plain-spoken, decades-old argument for why writing down what you're building first saves enormous pain later. You'll recognize the Hacky Hours philosophy in it.

What you just learned

  • Hacky Hours is a community framework (and Claude Code skill) from Empathetech for building real software with an LLM.
  • The core idea: you drive the product; Claude helps you build it, but it needs your direction.
  • It runs in five steps — Ideate, Design, Roadmap, Build, Iterate — backed by a team of twelve stakeholder-role agents.
  • Each agent is a doorway into a real discipline; defaults lean free, simple, and privacy-preserving.
  • It's for everyone, doing more of the process for people without the background — and it gives direction, not guarantees.
