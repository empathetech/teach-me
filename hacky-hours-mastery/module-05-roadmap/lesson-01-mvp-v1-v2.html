MVP vs. V1 vs. V2+ (Step 3)

You've designed something you're excited about. Now comes the moment that protects every excited builder from burning out: deciding what to build first, and being honest that the first version is supposed to be small. Let's make peace with that.

In Step 2 you and the team wrote down what your app should do. That list is almost always bigger than what you can — or should — build right away. Step 3, the Roadmap, is where you take every feature from your design documents and decide when each one gets built.

What "MVP" actually means

MVP stands for minimum viable product. In plain terms: the smallest version of your app that proves the core idea is worth building at all. It is supposed to be incomplete. That's not a compromise — it's the point.

The Hacky Hours skill describes the MVP this way:

From Step 3 — Roadmap "MVP — the smallest version that proves the core value proposition. Push back hard. 'Can the product prove its value without this?' If yes, it's not MVP."

A useful test for every feature: imagine your app without it. If the core idea still works and someone could still get value, then that feature is not MVP. It has a home later — just not now.

The three buckets: MVP, V1, V2+

During Step 3, the team — led by the Product agent, with the Architect weighing in on sequencing — walks through every feature and drops each into one of three buckets:

  • MVP — the bare core that proves the idea. Deliberately small.
  • V1 — the MVP plus what it needs to be genuinely useful to real people, not just a proof.
  • V2+ — genuinely valuable ideas that are not required yet. The dream isn't cut; it's parked.

One detail worth noticing: the skill asks you to write milestones as outcomes, not tasks. "Users can complete a purchase" is an outcome. "Implement checkout UI" is a task. Outcomes keep you focused on what a person can actually do.

Every feature gets a home Sorting into V2+ is not the same as deleting. The roadmap is finished when "every planned feature has a home" — MVP, V1, or V2+. You are sequencing, not abandoning.

Version numbers, in plain language

Software is labeled with version numbers like 0.1.0 or 2.3.1. This follows a shared convention called semantic versioning, written as MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH. You don't need the full rules to start — two anchors matter for your roadmap:

  • Your MVP ships as 0.1.0. The leading zero is a quiet, honest signal: "this is early, and things may still change."
  • Your V1 ships as 1.0.0. Reaching 1.0.0 says "this is the real thing now."

An MVP at 0.1.0 isn't a lesser version of your app. It's the first honest step toward it.

Growing your planning judgment 🌱

Good next steps
  • Read the official spec for version numbers at semver.org — short, plain, and worth ten minutes.
  • Look at a real project's release history on GitHub and notice how versions climb from 0.x toward 1.0.0 and beyond.
  • Run /hacky-hours step 3 on your own idea and watch which features the Product agent challenges.

What you just learned

  • The MVP is the smallest version that proves your core idea — it's meant to be incomplete.
  • Step 3 sorts every feature into MVP, V1, or V2+ — no feature is deleted, just sequenced.
  • Milestones are written as outcomes ("users can book a walk"), not tasks.
  • Your MVP ships as 0.1.0; your V1 ships as 1.0.0.
  • If everything feels like MVP, that's the signal to cut — which is exactly the next lesson.
