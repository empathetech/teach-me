Cutting scope without losing the vision

"Cutting scope" sounds like giving up on your dream. It isn't. It's the skill that gets your dream into someone's hands instead of leaving it stuck in a planning document forever. Here's how to say "later" without losing the "someday."

7 min read hands-on grows: scope discipline

Most first projects don't fail because the idea was bad. They stall because the first version was too big to ever finish. Step 3 exists to prevent exactly that. The Hacky Hours skill states the rule plainly:

From Hacky Hours · Step 3 "Pushes back on scope creep — if the MVP feels huge, it's too big."

What scope creep feels like

Scope creep is the slow growth of "wouldn't it also be nice if…" until the first version quietly becomes the whole dream. It rarely feels like a mistake in the moment — each addition seems small and reasonable. The damage shows up later, as a build that never ends.

The skill gives you a concrete gut-check. After your MVP list is set, it asks: "how long do you realistically think this would take to build?" And it warns: "if the answer suggests months, the MVP is probably still too big."

"Later" is a real home, not a graveyard

The reason cutting scope doesn't hurt is the same reason from the last lesson: the roadmap finishes only when every planned feature has a home. A feature you move to V2+ is written down, sequenced, and waiting. It is not deleted.

The vision is safe Your ambitious idea lives in your design documents and your V1/V2+ buckets. Cutting scope decides the order you build in. It never decides whether something gets built at all.

The one question that does the work

You don't need a complicated framework to cut scope well. You need one honest question, asked of every feature:

"Can the product prove its value without this?"

If the answer is yes, the feature is not MVP. Move it out. This single question, applied without flinching, will keep your first version small enough to actually ship — which is the only way you ever learn whether the idea works.

How to say "later" out loud

Saying "later" gets easier when you say it specifically. Compare these:

  • ❌ "Let's cut reviews." — sounds like loss.
  • ✅ "Reviews are a V1 feature — we'll add them right after people can book." — sounds like a plan.

Growing your scope discipline 🌱

Good next steps
  • Read the original "Minimum Viable Product" idea from Eric Ries' Lean Startup principles — it's where the "build to learn" mindset comes from.
  • Take your own idea and force yourself to write an MVP of three features or fewer. Notice how uncomfortable — and clarifying — that is.
  • Run /hacky-hours step 3 and let the Product agent challenge your MVP. Saying "you're right, that's V1" is a skill you build by reps.

What you just learned

  • "If the MVP feels huge, it's too big" — and a months-long MVP is the warning sign.
  • Cutting scope sets the order you build in; it never deletes the dream.
  • One question does most of the work: "Can the product prove its value without this?"
  • Say "later" specifically — name the version a feature belongs to.
