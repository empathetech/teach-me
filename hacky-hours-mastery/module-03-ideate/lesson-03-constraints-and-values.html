Constraints & values

The five Ws describe what you're building. The last part of Step 1 asks something different: what you're willing to do to build it. Three quiet questions — about licensing, privacy, and infrastructure — that shape everything downstream.

Why Step 1 asks these now

After you finish the five Ws, the skill instructs 📊 Maya Tanaka to "always ask the Constraints & Values questions before moving to Step 2." This isn't paperwork. These answers feed directly into later files — a LICENSING.md and an ARCHITECTURE.md in the Design step.

The reason they come up now is that they're cheap to decide on paper and expensive to unwind once built. Choosing to collect someone's phone number is a sentence today; removing it after launch can mean migrations, notifications, and apologies.

The three questions

Here's what Maya actually asks, with the spirit behind each:

1. Licensing

"Do you want your code to be open source — meaning others can see, use, and build on it — or do you want to keep it private? Are you planning to charge money for it?" There's no wrong answer; the point is to decide on purpose. Open source means your code is publicly readable and reusable under a license you choose.

2. Privacy

"How much user data does this product really need to collect? Less is almost always safer, cheaper, and easier to comply with legally." Notice the framing: not "what could you collect" but "what does it really need." Those are very different questions.

3. Infrastructure

"Do you want someone else to manage the servers, or are you comfortable managing your own?" Infrastructure is the machinery your app runs on — servers, databases, hosting. Letting someone else run it (a managed service) is usually simpler and safer for a first project.

How they connect to the framework's values

These three questions aren't random — each maps to one of Hacky Hours' guiding values, quoted here directly:

From the Hacky Hours values "Free before paid." · "Privacy-preserving before data-rich. Collect the minimum data needed." · "Less infrastructure before more. Prefer a simple hosted solution over a self-managed one."

For NeighborBoard, those values point somewhere concrete. Open source, because a neighborhood tool benefits from being shareable. Collect almost nothing — maybe just an event title, date, and a poster's first name, no accounts at first. And lean on a simple managed host rather than running a server yourself. Each choice is the lighter, safer one.

"The data you don't collect is the data you can never leak, lose, or be asked to hand over."

Your call, your responsibility Deciding what personal data to collect is genuinely yours to make — not Maya's, not the framework's. If you do collect personal information, you take on real duties: protecting it, being honest about it, and in many places following privacy laws. None of that is a reason to be afraid of building. It's a reason to collect only what you truly need, so the responsibility you carry stays small.

When you're ready, the real questions come up at the end of Step 1, right after your five Ws:

Answer honestly, even if the answer is "I'm not sure yet." Maya can work with uncertainty — what she can't work with is a decision you made by accident.

Grow your judgment 🌱 Privacy thinking is a craft of its own. A great free starting point is the data-minimization principle — collect only what you need, keep it only as long as you need it. The GDPR explainer on data minimization lays it out in plain language, and the instinct it builds protects every user you'll ever have.

What you just learned

  • Step 1 ends with three Constraints & Values questions: licensing, privacy, infrastructure.
  • They map to the framework's values: free before paid, privacy-preserving, less infrastructure.
  • What data you collect is your call — and your responsibility; collecting less is usually safer.
  • These answers seed LICENSING.md and ARCHITECTURE.md in Step 2.
  • "I'm not sure yet" is fine; an accidental decision is not.
