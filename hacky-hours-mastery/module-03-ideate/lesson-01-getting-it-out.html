Getting the idea out of your head (Step 1)

Before anyone designs a screen or writes a line of code, there's a quieter first move: getting the thing in your head out where you can actually look at it. That's what Step 1 is for, and it asks almost nothing of you except honesty.

6 min read hands-on grows: product thinking

Welcome to the Ideate step. If you've ever had an idea that felt clear in your mind but fell apart the moment you tried to explain it, you already understand the problem this step solves. Ideas are slippery while they live only in your head. Step 1 gives them somewhere to land.

When you run /hacky-hours step 1, the framework opens a file called IDEATION.md. A .md file is just a plain text file (Markdown) — no special software needed, you can open it in any editor. This one is your free-writing space.

Messy is not just allowed — it's the point

Here's the most important thing to know before you start: there are no rules in IDEATION.md. Half-sentences are fine. Contradicting yourself is fine. Writing "I don't know who this is for yet" is fine. The skill itself describes this file in one line:

Straight from the skill "IDEATION.md is a free-writing space. No rules — just capture everything."

This is brainstorming, not commitment. Nothing you write here locks you into anything. You're not signing off on a plan; you're emptying your pockets onto the table so you can see what you actually have. The shaping comes later — and you won't do it alone.

Who helps you shape it Most of Step 1 is guided by 📊 Maya Tanaka — Product, the agent whose whole job is making sure you're building the right thing before you build any thing. She reads what you wrote and reflects it back to you. You don't have to arrive organized; that's her part.

Maya's opening prompts

To get you moving, Step 1 offers a few questions. You don't have to answer all of them, or any of them in order. They're starting points, not a form:

  • "Who is the first person you'd want to use this, and what would they do with it?"
  • "What problem have you personally experienced that this solves?"
  • "What would have to be true for this to be considered a success in one year?"

Notice that none of these ask how to build anything. That's deliberate. Step 1 cares about the problem and the person, not the technology. The instinct to jump straight to features and screens is exactly what this step asks you to resist for a little while.

"The idea doesn't have to be good yet. It just has to be out of your head."
