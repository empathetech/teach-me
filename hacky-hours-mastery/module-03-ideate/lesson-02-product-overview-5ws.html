The product overview & the 5 Ws

Once your messy free-write exists, the next move is to turn it into something a stranger could read and understand. That's the product overview — five plain questions that quietly decide how smoothly the rest of your project goes.

What the product overview is

After your free-writing in IDEATION.md, 📊 Maya Tanaka helps you synthesize it into a second file: PRODUCT_OVERVIEW.md. Where IDEATION.md was a pile of raw thoughts, this one is tidy. It answers five questions — the classic five Ws: Who, What, Where, When, and Why.

The skill is clear about what "done" looks like for this step:

The bar for a finished overview "Someone unfamiliar with the project could read PRODUCT_OVERVIEW.md and understand what's being built." That's the test. Not "impressive" — just understandable.

Maya doesn't fire all five questions at once. The skill instructs her to "go one W at a time, ask focused questions, and reflect the user's words back to them." It's a calm conversation, not an interrogation.

The five Ws, one at a time

Here's what each W is actually asking, in plain terms:

  • Who — the target audience, stated specifically. The skill warns against the answer "anyone." A real audience has edges.
  • What — what the product does, and what form it takes (an app? a site? a tool?).
  • Where — the platform: mobile, web, desktop, an API, and so on.
  • When — a rough timeline or sense of priority. "No rush" is a valid answer.
  • Why — the problem it solves and why that matters. This is the heart of it.
Why "Who" can't be "everyone" A product built for everyone tends to delight no one, because every design choice is a tug-of-war between people who want opposite things. Naming a specific first user gives every later decision a clear tie-breaker: does this help them?

Read that and you can picture the whole thing — who it's for, what it is, and why it should exist. That's the entire goal. Notice "Who" names an actual street, not "communities." Notice "Where" already leans toward the framework's less infrastructure value: a phone browser, no app to install.

Why clarity now saves pain later

It's tempting to skip this and start building. But every later step leans on this file. The Design step reads your "What" and "Where" to choose how things should work. The Roadmap step uses your "When" to decide what comes first. And whenever the team faces a choice, your "Why" is the tie-breaker.

A fuzzy overview doesn't disappear — it just turns into expensive confusion later, when changing course means redoing work instead of editing a sentence. Spending twenty calm minutes here is the cheapest course-correction you'll ever get.

"A sentence is cheap to rewrite. A half-built feature aimed at the wrong person is not."

What you just learned

  • PRODUCT_OVERVIEW.md answers five Ws: Who, What, Where, When, Why.
  • "Done" means a stranger could read it and understand what you're building.
  • Maya goes one W at a time and reflects your own words back to you.
  • "Who" should be specific — "everyone" is not an audience.
  • Clarity here is the cheapest course-correction in the whole project.
