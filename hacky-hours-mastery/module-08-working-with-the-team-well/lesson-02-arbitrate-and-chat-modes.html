Arbitrate & chat modes when agents disagree

A real team doesn't always agree — and neither does this one. Security might want more checks while Product wants to ship. That tension is healthy. This lesson is about how you, the person in charge, settle it.

8 min read hands-on grows: decision-making

When two agents pull in different directions, that's not a bug — it's the point of having different roles. Your job isn't to make them agree. It's to decide, with their reasoning in front of you.

Why disagreement is good

Imagine you're about to ship a feature. Security — Alex — wants two more input checks before it goes out. Product — Maya — points out you promised this to users on Friday. Both are right from where they sit. The framework calls the way you resolve this arbitration: settling a disagreement between roles.

You run it like this, with a mode and a short description of the topic:

You always make the final call None of these modes picks a winner for you. The framework is explicit that no mode auto-decides — it surfaces the disagreement and the trade-offs, and you choose. The modes just change how much detail you see before you do.

The three arbitrate modes

The mode is the second word. It controls how deep the conversation goes — and how much time and cost it spends.

decide — the cheapest and the default. Each role states its position in a few words: what it wants, why, and the cost if it loses. You read them side by side and choose. Fast and clear when you already have a sense of your preference and just need the trade-offs laid out.
resolve — the middle option. First you name the high-level concerns that matter ("we ship in two weeks", "don't break user trust"). Then each role proposes a resolution weighed against those concerns. Use this when the roles would propose the wrong thing without knowing what you care about.
watch — the richest and most expensive. Two or more roles actually talk to each other while you observe the back-and-forth. It ends when they converge or when you step in. Reserve this for genuinely hard disagreements where you want to see how the disciplines reason against each other. It's meant to be rare.

Whichever mode you pick, arbitration always writes an ADR — an Architecture Decision Record, a short note capturing what was decided and why. As the framework puts it, this matters "otherwise the decision evaporates and the team will re-litigate it next month."

Team chat: off, minimal, full

Arbitration is naturally a multi-voice moment, which is a good time to mention a separate setting: how chatty the team is on screen during any multi-role work. You control it with:

  • off — you see the outcome with minimal role dialogue. Even then, positions are still clearly attributed to roles during arbitration, because that's the whole point of it.
  • minimal — short, attributed turns. This is the default, and a good balance.
  • full — every voice, every turn. Great for learning how the team thinks; more to read.

A guiding rule from the framework: "No tokens for tokens' sake — every voice turn must add information."

The decision is yours to understand The agents give you sharp, role-specific arguments — but they can't know your users, your legal exposure, or your risk tolerance the way you do. An arbitration that lands on "ship now" is a recommendation, not a guarantee that shipping is safe. For anything that touches real people's money or data, the responsibility to slow down and verify stays with you.

Going deeper 🌱

The ADRs that arbitration produces are a genuine professional practice — worth understanding beyond this course.

Good next steps
  • Read what Architecture Decision Records are and why teams keep them: adr.github.io. Every arbitration writes one.
  • Next time two agents disagree in your own project, try decide first — it's cheap, and you'll learn whether you needed more.

What you just learned

  • Disagreement between roles is healthy; /hacky-hours arbitrate <mode> <topic> is how you settle it.
  • decide = quick positions side by side; resolve = roles propose against your concerns; watch = roles converse while you observe.
  • You always make the final call — no mode auto-picks a winner — and every arbitration writes an ADR.
  • team chat off | minimal | full controls how visible the team is; minimal is the default.
