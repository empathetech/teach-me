Teaching your team & exporting your docs

Two ideas close out this module. First: your team can actually learn from you over time. Second: the documents you've built aren't trapped in this folder — they're designed to graduate into a real team's knowledge base.

Your team learns: team update

When you finish a multi-role task, the framework quietly collects any feedback you gave — like "Alex, lead with blast radius before the threat model." That feedback sits as a draft until you promote it. The command that promotes it is:

The framework describes this plainly: it is "how agents learn. Without it, agents are frozen at their starter prompts. With it, the team gets better the more you work together." It walks you through each pending change — you can accept, edit, reject, or defer it — and records the accepted ones permanently.

You review every change Nothing about your team changes silently. The framework's rule: "Never silently overwrite system prompts. Always show the diff and require explicit accept." Your feedback becomes part of the team only when you say so.

The rest of the team family

A few sibling commands round out how the team manages its own memory. You won't need them often, but it helps to know they exist:

  • team reflect — an agent looks back at its own track record and refreshes how it describes itself.
  • team resume — generates a "resume" for an agent, built from its profile, history, and your feedback.
  • team backfill — retroactively fills in an agent's history from your project's changelog and git log, so a team adopted partway through still has a past.

Graduating your docs: export

Throughout this course you've been told the artifacts are "team-grade" and can "leave home." The export verb is what makes that real. It bundles your project's documentation into a format you can drop into a real team's tools.

The two targets in version 4:

  • markdown-bundle — gathers your overview, design docs, roadmap, decisions and more into a single clean markdown file. The framework builds a table of contents, fixes the cross-links between docs, and leaves out the messy internal notes. Paste it anywhere markdown is accepted.
  • html-bundle — a static website of the same docs. In version 4.0.1 the framework will point you toward the markdown route plus a site generator as the recommended path, and it tells you so honestly.

The point of export: the work you did solo can walk into a real team and stand on its own.

An export is a snapshot, not a sign-off A bundle is only as accurate as the docs behind it — and those docs were written with AI help. If you hand them to real colleagues or rely on them for something consequential, read them yourself first. Exporting doesn't transfer responsibility for what they say; that's still yours.

Three more verbs in one line each

  • feedback — capture friction you hit during a session, stored locally only (it never leaves your machine on its own).
  • issue — submit a GitHub issue to Empathetech; opt-in, so it only happens when you choose.
  • meta — cluster your local feedback into suggested improvements to the framework itself.

Going deeper 🌱

Good next steps
  • After your next build, run /hacky-hours team update and read each pending change before accepting — it's the habit that makes the team genuinely yours.
  • Try /hacky-hours export markdown-bundle on a finished project and open the result. Seeing your own docs render cleanly is satisfying.
  • The framework's own thinking lives in the docs repo: github.com/empathetech/hacky-hours-docs.

What you just learned

  • team update promotes your session feedback so agents genuinely learn — and you review every change.
  • team reflect / resume / backfill help the team manage its own memory.
  • export markdown-bundle | html-bundle graduates your docs into a real team's knowledge base.
  • feedback (local), issue (opt-in to Empathetech), and meta (improve the framework) round things out.
