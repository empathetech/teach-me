The twelve agents

When you run /hacky-hours, you don't get one all-purpose assistant. You get a team of twelve, each one focused on a different part of building software. This lesson introduces all of them by name — and shows you that each one is also a doorway into a real craft you can learn.

Where these names come from The twelve agents are part of Hacky Hours itself — they ship with the framework as a persistent team stored on your computer. We'll cover how to manage that team in the next few lessons. For now, just meet them.

Why twelve, not one

A real software team isn't one person wearing every hat. It's a product person who asks who the thing is for, an engineer who builds it, a security person who asks what could go wrong, and several more. Each role notices things the others would miss.

Hacky Hours gives a solo builder that same range. When you describe what you want, the right roles speak up at the right moments — so you end up with the kind of thinking a whole team would bring, even though it's just you.

What the team is and isn't These agents give you direction — good questions, sensible defaults, and a record of decisions. They are not a substitute for your own judgment, and they don't guarantee your software is correct, safe, or finished. You stay the decision-maker. Think of them as knowledgeable colleagues, not an autopilot.

The framework groups the twelve into four clusters. Here they are.

Shaping the idea — what are we building, and for whom?

  • 📊 Maya Tanaka — Product. Figures out who the software is for and what problem it actually solves, before any code exists. Her craft is product management.
  • 🎨 Felix Okafor — Design. How the thing looks, and more importantly how it feels to use. His craft is UX design (user experience).
  • 🏗️ Priya Chen — Architect. The overall shape: what pieces exist, how they connect, which technologies to pick (and avoid). Her craft is software architecture.

Building it — making it real and reasonably solid

  • 🖥️ Marcus Rivera — Frontend. Everything the user touches in the browser: screens, buttons, forms. His craft is frontend engineering.
  • ⚙️ Sam Park — Backend. The server and logic users never see that makes everything work. His craft is backend engineering.
  • 🛡️ Alex Davies — Security. How things could go wrong: leaked secrets, bad input, who can reach what. His craft is application security — and he gives direction, never a guarantee.

Making it work for real people — running, testing, access

  • 🚀 Jordan Kim — Ops. Getting your app off your laptop and onto the internet, then keeping it running. His craft is operations and deployment.
  • 🔍 Emma Wright — QA. Deliberately trying to break things before your users do. Her craft is quality assurance — testing and edge cases.
  • ♿ Lena Mwangi — Accessibility. Making sure people with disabilities can use what you build: screen readers, keyboard use, contrast. Her craft is accessibility, and it's central to Empathetech's mission.

The bigger picture — rights, data, and AI

  • 📜 Diego Romano — Licensing. Who may use your code, and whose code you may use. His craft is software licensing — the legal layer most beginners skip until it bites.
  • 📈 Yuki Nakamura — Data. What information your app stores and how the pieces relate. Her craft is data modeling — this is where databases live.
  • 🤖 Kai Patel — AI/ML. When (and whether) your app should use AI, and how to do it responsibly. Often the answer is "not yet." His craft is machine learning.

Try it: which agent would worry about this?

You don't need to memorize twelve names. What's worth building is the instinct for which kind of question belongs to which role — so when something feels off, you know who to call on. Read each concern, then pick the agent it belongs to.

Each agent is someone to learn from 🌱

Here's the quiet promise of this team. Every time an agent raises a concern, it's a window into a whole field that professionals spend careers in. You don't have to become an expert in all twelve. But over a few projects, you'll absorb how each one thinks — and that's how a non-engineer grows into a genuine builder.

A first step into the map Each discipline these agents represent is a recognized field of software practice. For a plain-language overview of what software engineering as a whole even covers, the MDN Learn Web Development path is a kind, free place to wander. Don't rush it — the point is curiosity, not completion.

What you just learned

  • Hacky Hours engages a team of twelve role-based agents, not one assistant.
  • They group into four clusters: shaping the idea, building it, making it work for real people, and the bigger picture.
  • Each agent is a doorway into a real discipline you can learn over time.
  • You don't memorize names — you build an instinct for which kind of question belongs to which role.
  • The full interactive directory lives at The Team.
