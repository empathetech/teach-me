Calibrating the team to you

Your team isn't a one-time thing that vanishes when you close the terminal. It's persistent — it lives on your computer, it's portable, and it can even learn from you over time. This lesson shows you how to shape it to fit how you work.

Where your team lives

Your team is stored at ~/.hacky-hours/teams/<name>/ on your own computer. The ~ just means your home folder. Each team is its own git repo — a self-contained, version-tracked folder — which means it's portable: you can move it to another machine or back it up like any other project.

Crucially, your team lives separately from any single project. You build one team and apply it to many projects, rather than starting fresh each time. The first time you use the framework, it sets up a team called default for you automatically.

From the framework "The team lives at ~/.hacky-hours/teams/<team-name>/ as its own git repo — separate from any project, portable across machines."

Tiers: how many agents you want

Not every project needs all twelve voices. A team has a tier that sets how many agents are on it. There are four:

  • solo — the leanest team, a small handful of agents. Good for a quick personal tool where you want speed over thoroughness.
  • lean — a bit more coverage, still small.
  • startup — a fuller roster without the whole crew.
  • full — all twelve agents. The default, and the most thorough.
An honest note on tiers In v4.0.1, the full tier is the one that's fully built. If you create a leaner team, the framework currently sets it up as full and tells you so, leaving you to trim agents by hand. So in practice today: full is the reliable choice, and leaner tiers are a direction the framework is growing toward. We mention this so nothing surprises you.

The commands you'll use

Here are the team commands worth knowing. None of the read-only ones change anything.

  • /hacky-hours team — shows your active team's roster at a glance.
  • /hacky-hours team list — lists all the teams you have and marks the active one.
  • /hacky-hours team show <id> — opens one agent's full profile (try team show alex).
  • /hacky-hours team new — creates a new team; it asks you for a name and tier.
  • /hacky-hours team switch <name> — binds the project you're in to a different team.

The team can learn from you 🌱

Here's the part that makes the team genuinely yours. As you work, you'll correct agents, set preferences, and make decisions. The command /hacky-hours team update takes those session changes and offers to save them into your team's repo — one at a time, so you accept, edit, or reject each.

Over many projects, your team gradually shapes itself around how you like to build. It starts generic and becomes personal. You're never locked in: because it's a git repo, every change is tracked and reversible.

"The team is the user's source of truth." — and that's why nothing gets written to it without your say-so.

You stay the editor Letting the team learn your preferences is a convenience, not a substitute for judgment. Review what team update proposes before accepting — the framework deliberately shows you each change rather than saving silently, precisely so the decisions stay yours.

What you just learned

  • Your team is persistent, stored at ~/.hacky-hours/teams/<name>/, and portable because it's a git repo.
