The eight values that guide everything

Behind every suggestion the twelve agents make, there's a small set of principles steering them. The framework calls them global values, and they exist to protect you — especially if you're not an engineer. Let's walk through all eight in plain language.

9 min read hands-on grows: good defaults
Straight from the source These eight values come word-for-word from the framework's own SKILL.md, where they're described as governing "every recommendation this framework makes… not defaults to override — they are the framework's design principles." We'll quote each one and then translate why it matters for you.

1. Human is the driver

From the values "You are the implementation team. The user is the C-suite. Ask first, act second. If something could be hard to undo, confirm first."

You are always in charge. The team proposes; you decide. Nothing big or hard-to-reverse happens without your sign-off. For a non-engineer, this is the value that keeps the tool from running off without you.

2. Free before paid

From the values "Recommend free/open-source tools and platforms before commercial ones. Flag costs clearly when paid options are relevant."

The team reaches for free, open-source options first and tells you plainly when something costs money. This keeps your project from quietly racking up bills — a real risk when you can't yet tell a free tier from a paid one.

3. Less infrastructure before more

From the values "Prefer a simple hosted solution over a self-managed one. A managed database over one the user has to operate. A single service over a microservices architecture."

Infrastructure is all the machinery your app runs on — servers, databases, services. The fewer moving parts you have, the fewer things can break and the less you have to understand. Simpler is friendlier.

4. Privacy-preserving before data-rich

From the values "Collect the minimum data needed. Flag data collection, storage, and third-party sharing as explicit decisions, not defaults."

The team won't quietly hoard people's information. Collecting less data means less to protect, less to leak, and fewer legal obligations. The principle of data minimization is a recognized privacy practice, not just a nicety.

5. Reduce attack surface

From the values "When two approaches accomplish the same goal, prefer fewer credentials, fewer external dependencies, and fewer moving parts."

Your attack surface is every place something could go wrong or be broken into. Fewer parts means fewer things to get wrong. Often the most secure choice is just the simpler one — though, as always, this is direction, not a guarantee.

6. Accessible by default

From the values "WCAG 2.1 AA is the right starting point for any UI. Don't wait for the user to ask. Semantic HTML before complex UI frameworks."

WCAG 2.1 AA is the common standard for making web content usable by people with disabilities. The team builds toward it from the start, rather than bolting it on later. This reflects Empathetech's mission directly.

7. Licensing hygiene from the start

From the values "Ask about license intent early. Flag dependency license conflicts before they become architectural problems."

A license sets the rules for who may use code. The team asks early what you intend, and warns you if a piece of borrowed code carries terms that clash with yours — before it's tangled into everything.

8. This is for everyone

From the values "From first-time builders to seasoned engineers. Never assume prior knowledge. Explain jargon the first time you use it. Keep the energy warm and the process human."

This is the value you're feeling right now. The team meets you where you are and explains things in plain language. You belong here whether or not you've ever written a line of code.

"They are not defaults to override — they are the framework's design principles."

Values give direction, not guarantees These principles steer the team toward safer, kinder, cheaper choices — but following them doesn't make your software automatically secure, private, accessible, or legally clear. They raise the floor; they don't remove your responsibility to check the things that matter for what you're building.
