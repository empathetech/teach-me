Your capstone build

Pick something small

The biggest mistake here is going big. Choose an idea you could actually finish — a single page, a small tool, one feature you've wished existed. In the framework's language this is your MVP: the minimum viable product, the smallest version that's genuinely useful.

Why small wins A small project lets you walk the entire flow and feel each step click into place. A huge one stalls in design and never ships. You can always iterate later — in fact, that's the whole point of the iterate loop you already learned. Start small, finish, then grow.

The full flow, end to end

Here's the sequence you'll run. Each command opens a step you've already studied — this is just stringing them together on one project.

A little orientation on each, in order:

  1. Ideate (Step 1) — free-write the idea, then shape it into a clear overview: who it's for, what it does, and the constraints that matter to you.
  2. Design (Step 2) — work through the design docs your project actually needs. Don't write all ten; write the ones that fit. The team applies safe defaults: free before paid, less data, fewer moving parts.
  3. Roadmap (Step 3) — sort every feature into MVP, V1, or later. If your MVP feels big, it's too big. Cut.
  4. Build (Step 4) — implement one piece at a time, and run the pre-merge checklist before calling anything done.
  5. Audit — /hacky-hours audit scans for leaked secrets and common problems before you go public.
  6. Ship (update) — publish the release. Then, if you like, /hacky-hours team update so your agents keep what they learned from this build.

Here's the pre-merge checklist again, because it's the habit that separates "it runs" from "it's ready":

The Step 4 pre-merge checklist
  • Implementation matches the relevant design document
  • No secrets, API keys, or credentials in code or commit history
  • All user input that crosses a trust boundary is validated
  • Error messages don't expose internal system state
  • Change has been manually tested against the relevant user journey

How far you've come

Take a second with this. When you started, building software meant typing a request into a model and accepting whatever came back — no plan, no review, no way to tell good from risky.

Now you can name who's in the room, ask the right role the right question, read a design doc, run an audit, settle a disagreement with an ADR, and decide — with reasons — what to ship. That's not a trick of the tool. That's judgment, and it's yours to keep.

You moved from prompting and hoping to making real, informed decisions. That's the whole course in one sentence.

An honest word before you ship

Direction, not guarantees — read this carefully Hacky Hours gives you direction: good defaults, the right questions, and a record of your decisions. It does not guarantee that what you build is correct, secure, legal, or safe — and neither does finishing this course. The agents are AI; they can be confidently wrong. The responsibility to review and understand what you ship stays with you, always. And for anything that touches real users' data — money, health, identity, anything that could hurt someone if it leaked — please get a qualified human to review it before it goes live. Knowing the edge of your own knowledge is the most important skill you've built here.

Where to go next 🌱

Finishing this course is a beginning, not an end. The way you keep growing is by building real things and reading past the parts you don't yet understand.

Keep going
  • Read the framework's own documentation and join the conversation: github.com/empathetech/hacky-hours-docs.
  • Keep an ADR habit — capture why you made each real decision: adr.github.io.
  • When this build is done, run /hacky-hours export markdown-bundle and keep your docs. They're proof of what you can do now.

What you just learned

  • The full flow runs ideate → design → roadmap → build → audit → ship, on one of your own ideas.
  • Start with a small MVP so you actually finish — you can always iterate.
  • The pre-merge checklist and audit are the habits that make a build "ready," not just "running."
  • Hacky Hours gives direction, not guarantees; you remain responsible for what you ship — especially anything with real users' data.
  • You've gone from prompting and hoping to directing a team and deciding on purpose. That's yours to keep.
