The architecture agent

The biggest, most expensive decisions in a build are the structural ones — what pieces exist and how they fit together. This lesson introduces the agent who helps you make them, and the one instinct that saves more first-time builders than any other: start simple.

What ARCHITECTURE.md captures

The skill says to write an ARCHITECTURE document when "the product has multiple systems or services." It answers three questions:

  • What pieces exist? For example: a website people visit, a place data is stored, maybe a background job that sends emails.
  • How do they connect? Which piece talks to which, and in what order.
  • What technology should you pick — or avoid? The specific tools that run each piece, chosen deliberately rather than by habit.

A quick definition: infrastructure means the servers, databases, and outside services your product runs on. A service is one running piece of your product that does a job — like the part that handles logins, or the part that processes payments.

Priya generates a Mermaid diagram of all this — a plain-text diagram GitHub turns into a picture — so the shape is something you can actually see, not just read.

The value: less infrastructure before more

Of the framework's guiding values, one steers nearly every architecture decision:

From the Hacky Hours values "Less infrastructure before more. Prefer a simple hosted solution over a self-managed one. A managed database over one the user has to operate. A single service over a microservices architecture."

In plain terms: when there's a simpler option that does the job, take it. A hosted service is one someone else runs for you, so you don't maintain the servers. A managed database is a place to store data that a company keeps running, backed up, and patched — you just use it. Both mean less for you to operate and fewer ways for things to break.

"Monolith first," in plain terms

You'll hear two words in architecture conversations. A monolith is a product built as one single service — everything in one place. Microservices means splitting that into many small separate services that talk to each other over the network.

Microservices sound advanced, and for very large teams they can help. But for a new or small project they add a lot of complexity — more pieces to deploy, more places to fail, more to coordinate — usually before you have any of the problems they solve. The widely-cited advice, from software writer Martin Fowler, is "monolith first": start as one service, and only split it later if and when you genuinely need to.

You can always add complexity later. It's far harder to remove it once something depends on it.

Learning more on your own 🌱

Architecture is a deep craft, but the core instinct — prefer the simplest thing that works — is something you can build today.

Good next steps
  • Read Monolith First by Martin Fowler — the short, plain-language case for starting simple.
  • On your next idea, ask Priya for both a "simplest version" and a "if it grows" version, and compare them.
  • Read how the agent thinks: /hacky-hours team show priya --prompt.

What you just learned

  • Priya Chen writes ARCHITECTURE.md: what pieces exist, how they connect, what tech to use or avoid.
  • The guiding value is "less infrastructure before more" — prefer hosted, managed, single-service.
  • "Monolith first" means start as one service and only split it later if you truly need to.
  • When a design feels elaborate, ask for the simplest version you could ship.
