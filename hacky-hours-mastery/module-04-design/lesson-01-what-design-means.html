What "design" means here (Step 2)

Before anyone writes code, Hacky Hours pauses to answer one question: how does this product actually work? Not how it looks — how it's built. This lesson walks you through what Step 2 produces and which parts of it your particular project needs.

8 min read hands-on grows: design literacy

You've shaped the idea in Step 1. Step 2 — Design — is where that idea becomes a plan detailed enough to build from. The skill describes its purpose in one plain sentence: "Define how the product works in enough detail to build it."

What Step 2 produces

Step 2 fans out across several of the team's agents at once. Each writes a short design document — a focused write-up of one part of how the product works. Together they answer the questions a builder would otherwise have to guess at: what pieces exist, what data is stored, who can do what, how it stays usable for everyone.

You don't need every document. The skill is clear about this — it starts by asking which documents your project actually needs, then only writes those. A small personal tool needs far less than a public app with accounts.

The ten design documents, in plain language

Here are all ten documents Step 2 can produce, with what each is for. Jargon is explained the first time it appears.

The Step 2 design documents
  • ARCHITECTURE — the big shape of the product: what pieces exist and how they connect. Lesson 2.
  • DATA_MODEL — what information the product stores and how those pieces relate. Lesson 5.
  • USER_JOURNEYS — the step-by-step paths people take through the product to get something done.
  • STYLE_GUIDE — the visual rules: colors, type, spacing, so the interface stays consistent.
  • ACCESSIBILITY — how the product stays usable for people with disabilities. Lesson 4.
  • MARKET_FIT — who the users are and why they'd choose this over alternatives.
  • BUSINESS_LOGIC — the rules and calculations specific to your product (for example, how a price or a score is worked out).
  • SECURITY_PRIVACY — what data you collect, who can reach what, and a baseline of safety checks. Lesson 3.
  • LICENSING — the legal terms for your code and the outside code it uses, decided early before problems pile up.
  • TESTING — how you'll check the product works, and what "done" means.

An interface, by the way, is simply the part of the product a person sees and interacts with — buttons, screens, forms. We'll use that word a lot.

Diagrams and safety-first defaults

Two things make these documents easier to live with than a wall of text.

First, the design agents generate Mermaid diagrams as they go. Mermaid is a way of writing a diagram as plain text that GitHub renders into an actual picture — boxes, arrows, and labels — automatically. You get a readable diagram without anyone hand-drawing one, and it stays in your project's text files.

Second, the agents apply safety-first defaults. Left to choose, they lean toward the cautious option: free before paid, less data before more, fewer outside dependencies before more. You can always override them — but the starting point is the safer one.

Design isn't decoration. It's the set of decisions that make the build predictable instead of a surprise.

When Step 2 is finished

The skill gives a refreshingly human definition of done for the whole step:

Done when "A new collaborator could understand how the product works." If a person who has never seen your project could read the design documents and grasp how it's built, Step 2 has done its job.

The next four lessons each sit with one design agent: the architecture agent (Lesson 2), the security agent (Lesson 3), the accessibility agent (Lesson 4), and the data agent (Lesson 5). By the end you'll know what each one is asking you and why.

Learning more on your own 🌱

None of this is required to finish the course — but understanding the shape of good design documents is how you grow from following a process into making the calls yourself.

What you just learned

  • Step 2 — Design — defines how the product works in enough detail to build it.
  • It produces up to ten focused design documents; your project only needs some of them.
  • The agents generate Mermaid diagrams and lean on safety-first defaults.
  • It's done when a new collaborator could understand how the product works.
  • Lessons 2–5 each cover one design agent: architecture, security, accessibility, data.
